# Strategic Narrative — Master Conversion Architecture

**DECISION-MAKING REFERENCE ONLY. Does not render anything.**

This is the master conversion-architecture doc. Read it before any
copy work. It contains: the core conversion thesis, belief chain,
awareness map, hero emotional arc, four hero copy variants, word-by-word
headline psychology, the canonical sample briefing output, demo chips
with prefilled prompts, the two-CTA strategy, and the "must-not-say" list.

---

## 1. The core conversion thesis

The buyer does not wake up wanting "AI financial automation." She
wakes up thinking:

- *Why does cash still feel tight?*
- *Can I afford to hire?*
- *Why does revenue look good but the business still feels fragile?*
- *Do I actually know what's happening, or am I just checking the bank
  account and hoping?*

That is the psychological entry point.

The strongest site will not begin with *"AI-powered spreadsheets for
small businesses."* It begins with:

> **Stop Running Your Business From Your Bank Balance.**

That headline names a behavior. It catches the buyer in the act.

The site has one job: **make the visitor feel the difference between
having financial data and having financial clarity.**

The conversion moment is when she thinks: *"That's exactly what I've
been doing. I've been running the business from my bank balance."*
Not when she sees the features. Not when she sees the price. Not when
she sees "AI-powered." Conversion starts the moment she feels
*exposed in a safe way.*

## 2. The product anatomy (use this framing rule everywhere)

The five-piece system, with the line that makes each piece make sense:

| Piece                              | What it is                       | The framing line                                |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Expert-built spreadsheet system    | The structure                    | *The spreadsheet is the structure.*             |
| Plaid bank connection              | The data pipe                    | *Plaid is the data pipe.*                       |
| AI-assisted organization           | The automation layer             | *AI is the automation layer.*                   |
| Bi-weekly plain-English briefings  | The interpretation layer         | *The briefing is the interpretation.*           |
| Monthly one-hour strategy call     | The strategic / human layer      | *The monthly call is the rhythm.*               |

**Rule**: never sell the parts. Always sell the system. The whole
becomes a *recurring financial operating system* — that's the category.

Internal positioning shorthand: *"A mini Goldman Sachs-style finance
desk for small businesses."* This phrase is powerful for internal
alignment. **It never appears in public copy.** Public copy uses
*"institutional-style financial clarity"* or *"investment-bank-style
financial intelligence."* See `brand-identity-architect.md` for the
full disclosure rule.

## 3. The belief chain (must be built in order)

Conversion is a sequence of beliefs, not a single headline. Move
Claire through each in order.

### Belief 1 — "They understand my real problem."
Pain recognition.

> Your bank account tells you what is left. It does not tell you what
> changed, what is risky, or what decision deserves your attention.

### Belief 2 — "This is not just another spreadsheet."
Category reframing.

> The spreadsheet is the structure. The briefing is the
> interpretation. The monthly call is the rhythm.

### Belief 3 — "This is not just software."
Competitor contrast.

> Dashboards show data. The Monthly Finance Desk helps explain meaning.
>
> Ramp helps control spend. QuickBooks manages accounting. Mercury
> manages banking. Bookkeepers keep records clean. The Monthly Finance
> Desk sits in the gap with structured visibility, written
> interpretation, and a monthly finance conversation.

### Belief 4 — "This is serious enough for my business."
Design, proof, specificity, qualification.

> Built for owner-led businesses that have outgrown messy
> spreadsheets, unclear reports, and once-a-year financial advice.

### Belief 5 — "I can try it safely."
Product-led preview.

> No bank connection required for the preview. Use demo data or
> rough non-sensitive numbers.

### Belief 6 — "$1,500/month makes sense."
Price anchoring.

> Less than hiring a CFO. More strategic than a template. More
> personal than a dashboard. More consistent than waiting until tax season.

### Belief 7 — "Applying is the right next step."
Selective language.

> Apply for the Monthly Finance Desk.
>
> *(Not "Buy now." Not "Checkout." Not "Submit.")*

If any belief is missing or out of order, conversion drops.

## 5. The hero emotional arc (five states)

The hero must move the visitor through these five states in order.

1. **Recognition** — *Stop Running Your Business From Your Bank Balance.* → "That is exactly what I do."
2. **Relief** — *Most owners have bank statements, bookkeeping reports, and spreadsheets.* → "I'm not the only one. I'm not being judged."
3. **Curiosity** — *Describe your business and what you want to understand about your numbers.* → "What would this say about me?"
4. **Trust / Safety** — *No bank connection required for the preview.* → "I can try this without exposing anything sensitive."
5. **Desire** — Sample briefing renders. → "I want this every two weeks for my real business."

Only after these five states should the premium "Apply" CTA become prominent.

---

## 6. The most important hero rule

> **The hero must not ask Claire to believe. It must make her experience.**

A normal hero says: *"We help you understand your numbers."*

A high-converting hero lets her type: *"I run an agency doing $90K/month
but cash feels tight."* Then shows: *"Revenue is growing, but expenses
are scaling faster than margin. Before hiring, review cash flow
stability and contractor concentration."*

That moment sells. The product stops being abstract and becomes a
preview of the relief she wants. **If a redesign breaks the
interactive preview, it breaks the conversion.**

## 8. Word-by-word hero psychology

Why each phrase in the canonical headline + subhead is chosen.

### "For serious small business owners"
Three jobs: qualifies the audience, flatters the right person, repels
bargain hunters. "Serious" prepares Claire for a higher-ticket offer.

### "Stop"
Stronger than "Start." Great conversion copy starts by naming the
thing the prospect wants to stop doing. Claire doesn't wake up wanting
financial clarity software. She wakes up wanting to stop feeling
anxious every time she checks cash.

### "Running Your Business"
Bigger than "managing your finances." "Managing your finances" sounds
like a task. "Running your business" sounds like leadership. The
phrase connects the financial problem to the whole company, not just
the finance department. Claire is buying a better way to lead.

### "From Your Bank Balance"
The sharpest part of the headline. It names the exact false source of
confidence. The bank balance is emotionally powerful because it is
simple — and misleading. It doesn't tell her what bills are coming,
what taxes are owed, whether expenses are creeping up, whether hiring
is safe, whether cash flow is improving, whether revenue quality is
strong, or whether profit is actually healthy. The phrase creates
productive discomfort: "I know I shouldn't be doing that."

### "Most owners have…"
Lowers shame. Instead of "You are doing it wrong," it says "Most
owners are in this situation." If Claire feels judged, she leaves. If
she feels understood, she continues.

### "Bank statements, bookkeeping reports, and spreadsheets"
Names the tools she already has. Prevents the "But I already have
QuickBooks / a bookkeeper / spreadsheets" objection. The copy
preempts: *"Yes. We know. That is exactly the problem. You have the
pieces, but not the interpretation rhythm."*

### "What they do not have…"
Reveals the missing piece. The best conversion copy doesn't just say
what the product does — it reveals the gap.

### "Recurring finance rhythm"
The category-creation phrase. Communicates repetition, discipline,
review, accountability, maturity, consistency. Also justifies the
subscription model: Claire isn't paying $1,500/month for a one-time
spreadsheet — she's paying for a rhythm that keeps finance from being
ignored.

### "The Monthly Finance Desk"
Stronger than "our platform." "Platform" is generic. "Monthly Finance
Desk" feels specific, premium, ownable. "Desk" implies analysis,
structure, professionalism, repetition, serious review. Much better
than "AI spreadsheet tool."

### "Turns your financial activity into…"
"Financial activity" is broader than "transactions" — it includes the
messy reality (money in, money out, payroll, unusual charges, upcoming
obligations). "Turns" communicates transformation: raw activity
becomes structured clarity.

### "Organized spreadsheets"
Keeps the offer concrete. Don't hide the spreadsheet component
completely — it's part of the product. But don't lead with it either.
"Organized spreadsheets" sounds like a useful layer inside a bigger
system. "Spreadsheet templates" sounds cheap.

### "Bi-weekly plain-English briefings"
"Reports" sounds cold or generic. "Briefings" sound executive and
interpreted. "Plain-English" handles the fear that finance will feel
confusing. "Bi-weekly" creates consistency.

### "Monthly strategy review"
The premium human layer. Without this the product risks sounding like
software. Frame the monthly call as the moment the owner steps out of
chaos and reviews the business like a CEO. The emotional anchor:
*"I have someone to talk to about this."*

## 9. The interactive hero experience

### Default state
- Left side: headline, subheadline, prompt, CTAs, demo chips, trust microcopy.
- Right side: empty briefing preview with soft loading skeleton and
  section labels. "No bank connection required" reassurance.
- Should create curiosity without asking for commitment.

### User types or selects a chip
Right panel changes from empty to "Analyzing sample financial
activity…" with restrained loading lines (see `scroll-motion.md`):

- *Reading revenue movement…*
- *Reviewing expense pattern…*
- *Checking cash pressure…*
- *Preparing plain-English briefing…*

**Forbidden**: "Unlocking AI magic…", "Supercharging your data…",
"Revolutionizing your finances…". Calm beats excitement.

### Sample output appears
See §11 for the canonical output. Specific enough to feel real, but
never claims actual financial advice.

### CTA after value
Only after the sample renders does the primary "Apply for the Monthly
Finance Desk" CTA appear more prominently. Secondary: "Get the Free
Templates." Microcopy: *"Start with the free templates, or apply to
have your financial system organized, briefed, and reviewed monthly."*

## 10. Hero copy variants (A/B test menu)

When the site is rebuilt, these four variants are the test slate.
Default is Variant A.

### Variant A — Pain-First (default)
- **Headline**: *Stop Running Your Business From Your Bank Balance*
- **Subhead**: *Your bank account tells you what is left. It does not tell you what changed, what is risky, or what decision deserves your attention. The Monthly Finance Desk turns your financial activity into plain-English insight every two weeks.*
- **CTA**: *Generate Sample Finance Briefing*
- **Best for**: cold, broad traffic. Highest pain-recognition.

### Variant B — Premium Authority
- **Headline**: *Institutional-Style Financial Clarity for Owner-Led Businesses*
- **Subhead**: *A premium monthly finance subscription combining expert-built spreadsheets, Plaid-powered automation, bi-weekly financial briefings, and a monthly strategy review — without hiring a full-time finance team.*
- **CTA**: *Apply for the Monthly Finance Desk*
- **Best for**: warm, high-income visitors. Referral and LinkedIn traffic.

### Variant C — Outcome-First
- **Headline**: *Know What Your Numbers Are Trying to Tell You*
- **Subhead**: *Most small business owners have financial data everywhere. We turn that data into organized spreadsheets, written interpretation, and a monthly strategy rhythm so you can make decisions with more clarity.*
- **CTA**: *Test the Finance Desk*
- **Best for**: SEO traffic landing from "what do my financial statements mean" queries.

### Variant D — Category-Creation
- **Headline**: *Your Monthly Finance Room*
- **Subhead**: *A recurring financial review system for serious business owners: structured spreadsheets, AI-assisted organization, bi-weekly plain-English reports, and monthly strategic review.*
- **CTA**: *Build My Finance Room*
- **Best for**: brand-led campaigns, podcast attribution, founder-network traffic.

### Prediction
A wins cold traffic. B wins warm/referral. C wins SEO. D wins brand
campaigns. Run A as control.

## 11. The canonical sample briefing output

Any future copywriter must match this voice. Specific. Plain-English.
Names patterns and risks. Does not claim certainty. Always ends with
questions for the monthly call.

> **Sample Bi-Weekly Finance Briefing**
>
> **Cash Movement**
> Your demo business brought in $92,400 this month and spent $78,900.
> Cash increased, but most of the gain came from delayed vendor
> payments, not stronger operating margin.
>
> **Revenue Trend**
> Revenue is up 14% compared with the previous period. The increase
> appears concentrated in one client segment, which may create risk
> if the trend does not continue.
>
> **Expense Pattern**
> Software, subcontractor, and owner-discretionary expenses are
> increasing faster than revenue. These categories should be reviewed
> before adding new fixed costs.
>
> **Decision to Review**
> Before hiring again, review whether current monthly cash flow can
> support another payroll obligation without relying on delayed vendor
> payments.
>
> **Questions for Monthly Call**
> Should the next move be hiring, expense cleanup, pricing review, or
> cash reserve planning?

This output sells because it feels like *interpretation*, not reporting.

## 13. The two-CTA strategy

The homepage should not force everyone into the same action.

### High-intent CTA
**Apply for the Monthly Finance Desk**

"Apply" is better than "Buy" because the offer is premium,
advisory-led, and trust-based. "Apply" signals selectivity and
qualification. "Buy" signals e-commerce.

### Low-intent CTA
**Get Free Templates**

Captures buyers who aren't ready for $1,500/month yet. The free
templates are the beginning of the funnel — they let Claire experience
the structure, the thinking, the gaps in her current system, and the
value of upgrading.

Both CTAs should exist throughout the page. Never strip the
free-template path to force premium conversion — it cuts off the
nurture funnel.

## 14. What the hero must NOT say

Forbidden, even in early drafts:

- "AI-powered spreadsheet automation for small businesses"
- "Manage your finances smarter"
- "Take control of your business finances"
- "Get better financial insights"
- "All-in-one finance dashboard"
- "Book a call" (premature)
- "Learn more" (vague)
- "Get started" (generic)
- "Unlock financial clarity" (cliché)
- "Supercharge your numbers" (hype)
- "Revolutionary finance platform" (overclaim)

These are too generic. They don't create pain recognition. Worse, the
competitor field is already crowded with AI + accounting + insights
copy, so the site can't win by sounding like everyone else.

## 16. Final conversion principle

Claire converts when the homepage makes her feel three things at once:

1. **Exposed** — "I have been running the business from my bank balance."
2. **Relieved** — "This is common, and there is a more mature way to handle it."
3. **Curious** — "I want to see what this would say about my business."

The hero's job is not to explain every feature. The hero's job is to
create the thought:

> *"I want my own version of that finance briefing."*

Once she thinks that, the application CTA becomes natural.

---

## 17. North-Star principle

> **Show the value before asking for the commitment.**

The real conversion target is not an application submission. The real
conversion target is the thought *"I want my own version of that
finance briefing."* Once Claire thinks that, the site has done its job.
